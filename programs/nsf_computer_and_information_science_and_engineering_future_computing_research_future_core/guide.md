# NSF Computer and Information Science and Engineering: Future Computing Research (Future CoRe)

## 1. Executive Summary

The NSF Directorate for Computer and Information Science and Engineering (CISE) supports research and education projects that develop new knowledge across all aspects of computing, communications, and information science and engineering. The Future Computing Research (Future CoRe) program encompasses eleven distinct research areas, fostering both single-program and interdisciplinary research. This solicitation aims to advance the mathematical, scientific, and technological foundations of computing, communication, hardware, software, and emerging technologies; enhance understanding and development of various computing systems; and deepen the understanding of the inter-related roles of people, computers, and information. The program encourages proposals from EPSCoR-eligible institutions to broaden participation in STEM.

## 2. Program Overview

The CISE Future Computing Research (Future CoRe) program supports transformative research and education projects across the following eleven core programs:

*   **Algorithmic Foundations (AF)**: Supports transformative projects in the theory of algorithms and computational complexity, characterized by algorithmic innovation and rigorous analysis.
*   **Communications and Information Foundations (CIF)**: Supports foundational research addressing the theoretical underpinnings of information acquisition, transmission, and processing in communications and information processing systems.
*   **Computer Systems Research (CSR)**: Supports the advancement and holistic design and development of integrated software and hardware computing systems, including classical and quantum elements, to enable an integrated, cohesive, intelligent computational ecosystem.
*   **Computing Education Research (CER)**: Supports transformative projects that inform our understanding of the effective teaching and learning of computing skills and concepts at all levels to contribute to the development of, and pathways toward, a robust workforce well prepared for careers in CISE fields.
*   **Cyber-Physical Systems Foundations and Connected Communities (CPS)**: Supports convergent research on intelligent, engineered systems, built from the seamless integration of computation with physical components within natural and built environments. It seeks foundational and translational advances in all areas of cyber-physical systems, including artificial intelligence for cyber-physical systems, and encourages multidisciplinary socio-technical innovation.
*   **Foundations of Emerging Technologies (FET)**: Supports foundational research at the intersection of computing and biological systems, nanoscale science and engineering, quantum information science, and other promising disruptive technologies supporting novel computing/communication models.
*   **Human-Centered Computing (HCC)**: Supports research in human-computer interaction, integrating across fields including computing, information, social and behavioral sciences, to (re)design technologies that amplify human capabilities, and understand how human, technical, and contextual aspects of computing and communication systems shape their benefits, effects, and risks.
*   **Information Integration and Informatics (III)**: Supports research on computational approaches to the full data lifecycle to maximize the utility of information resources.
*   **Networking Technology and Systems (NeTS)**: Supports research that advances intelligent communication network systems, including wired, wireless, Internet, internet of things, quantum, bio-inspired, nano, and other networks, as well as networks that span multiple domains and scales. It explores their fundamental understanding, properties and trade-offs, and develops innovative tools and techniques, including artificial intelligence (AI), for designing, building, measuring and managing future communication network systems and services.
*   **Robust Intelligence (RI)**: Supports computational research to understand and enable intelligent systems in complex, realistic contexts.
*   **Software and Hardware Foundations (SHF)**: Supports foundational research in the design, verification, operation, utilization and evaluation of computer hardware and software through novel approaches, robust theories, high-leverage tools, and lasting principles.

Research that fits within a single program and interdisciplinary research that spans more than one of these programs are welcome. NSF has a mandate to broaden participation in science and engineering, with particular emphasis on groups and regions that traditionally have not participated fully.

## 4. Eligibility

**Who May Submit Proposals:**
Proposals may only be submitted by the following:

*   **Institutions of Higher Education (IHEs)**: Two- and four-year IHEs (including community colleges) accredited in, and having a campus located in the US, acting on behalf of their faculty members.
    *   *Special Instructions for International Branch Campuses of US IHEs*: If the proposal includes funding for an international branch campus (including through sub-awards and consultant arrangements), the proposer must explain the benefit(s) to the project of performance at the international branch campus and justify why the activities cannot be performed at the US campus.
*   **Non-profit, non-academic organizations**: Independent museums, observatories, research laboratories, professional societies, and similar organizations located in the U.S. that are directly associated with educational or research activities.
*   **Tribal Nations**: An American Indian or Alaska Native tribe, band, nation, pueblo, village, or community that the Secretary of the Interior acknowledges as a federally recognized tribe pursuant to the Federally Recognized Indian Tribe List Act of 1994, 25 U.S.C. §§ 5130-5131.

**Who May Serve as PI:**
There are no restrictions or limits.

**Limit on Number of Proposals per Organization:**
There are no restrictions or limits.

**Limit on Number of Proposals per PI or co-PI:**
An individual may not serve as a PI, co-PI, or Senior/Key Personnel on **more than two proposals** submitted within any consecutive 12-month period across *all* Future CoRe programs listed in this solicitation.

*   Proposals submitted to previous versions of CISE core Programs (NSF 24-589), Cyber-Physical Systems (NSF 24-581), Smart and Connected Communities (NSF 25-527), or any other NSF funding opportunities do not count towards this limit.
*   This eligibility constraint will be strictly enforced. Any proposal exceeding this limit at the time of submission for any PI, co-PI, or Senior/Key Personnel will be returned without review. No exceptions will be made.
*   Proposals withdrawn prior to merit review or returned without review by NSF will not count against this limit.

## 5. Award Size & Budget

*   **Anticipated Type of Award**: Standard Grant or Continuing Grant.
*   **Maximum Budget**: Up to **$1,000,000** for the entire project.
*   **Maximum Duration**: Up to **4 years**.
*   **Typical Projects**: Approximately **$150,000 to $250,000 per year** and typically **3 to 4 years** in duration.
*   **Annual Spending**: Projects are discouraged from exceeding **$300,000 in any single year**.
*   **Anticipated Funding Amount**: Up to **$280,000,000** each year, dependent upon the availability of funds.
*   **Estimated Number of Awards**: 400 to 600.
*   **Cost Sharing Requirements**: Inclusion of voluntary committed cost sharing is **prohibited**.
*   **Indirect Cost (F&A) Limitations**: Not Applicable.
*   **Other Budgetary Limitations**: Not Applicable.

## 6. How Proposals are Reviewed

Proposals are reviewed based on National Science Board approved merit review criteria, which apply to all NSF proposals.

**Merit Review Principles:**

1.  All NSF projects should be of the highest quality and have the potential to advance, if not transform, the frontiers of knowledge.
2.  NSF projects, in the aggregate, should contribute more broadly to achieving societal goals ("Broader Impacts"). These may be accomplished through the research itself, directly related activities, or complementary activities.
3.  Meaningful assessment and evaluation of NSF funded projects should be based on appropriate metrics, considering the correlation between broader impacts and resources. PIs are expected to be accountable for carrying out described activities, with clearly stated goals, specific descriptions, and a plan to document outputs.

**Merit Review Criteria:**

Reviewers will evaluate proposals against two criteria, both of which must be given full consideration:

1.  **Intellectual Merit**: Encompasses the potential to advance knowledge.
2.  **Broader Impacts**: Encompasses the potential to benefit society and contribute to the achievement of specific, desired societal outcomes.

For both criteria, reviewers will consider:

*   What is the potential for the proposed activity to:
    *   Advance knowledge and understanding within its own field or across different fields (Intellectual Merit)?
    *   Benefit society or advance desired societal outcomes (Broader Impacts)?
*   To what extent do the proposed activities suggest and explore creative, original, or potentially transformative concepts?
*   Is the plan for carrying out the proposed activities well-reasoned, well-organized, and based on a sound rationale? Does the plan incorporate a mechanism to assess success?
*   How well qualified is the individual, team, or organization to conduct the proposed activities?
*   Are there adequate resources available to the PI (either at the home organization or through collaborations) to carry out the proposed activities?

Broader impacts include, but are not limited to: full participation of women, persons with disabilities, and underrepresented minorities in STEM; improved STEM education; increased public scientific literacy; improved well-being of individuals; development of a diverse STEM workforce; increased partnerships; improved national security; increased economic competitiveness; and enhanced infrastructure for research and education.

Reviewers will also review the Data Management and Sharing Plan, Mentoring Plan, and the optional Broadening Participation in Computing Plan, as appropriate.

**Review and Selection Process:**

*   Proposals will be reviewed by **Ad hoc Review and/or Panel Review**.
*   Reviewers provide a summary rating and narrative.
*   The NSF Program Officer considers reviewer advice and formulates a recommendation.
*   Recommendations are reviewed by the cognizant Division Director.
*   Approved proposals are forwarded to the Division of Grants and Agreements or the Division of Acquisition and Cooperative Support for review of business, financial, and policy implications.
*   A Grants and Agreements Officer performs final processing and issuance of the award. Only a Grants and Agreements Officer can make commitments or authorize expenditure of funds.
*   NSF strives to inform proposers of award or declination decisions within six months of the deadline/target date or receipt date, whichever is later.
*   PIs receive verbatim copies of reviews (excluding reviewer identities) and an explanation of the funding decision.

## 7. Application Requirements

Proposals must be submitted in accordance with the NSF Proposal & Award Policies & Procedures Guide (PAPPG) that is in effect for the relevant due date.

**Submission Methods:**

*   **Research.gov**: Full Proposals submitted via Research.gov must follow PAPPG guidelines. Collaborative proposals submitted as separate submissions from multiple organizations must use Research.gov.
*   **Grants.gov**: Full Proposals submitted via Grants.gov must follow the NSF Grants.gov Application Guide.

**Proposal Titles:**

Proposal titles must begin with a **bolded acronym** indicating the most relevant Future CoRe program, followed by a colon, then the project title.

*   **AF**: Algorithmic Foundations
*   **CIF**: Communications and Information Foundations
*   **CSR**: Computer Systems Research
*   **CER**: Computing Education Research
*   **CPS-FR**: Cyber-Physical Systems Foundations and Connected Communities (Foundational Research track)
*   **CPS-CIR**: Cyber-Physical Systems Foundations and Connected Communities (Community-Inspired Research track)
*   **FET**: Foundations of Emerging Technologies
*   **HCC**: Human-Centered Computing
*   **III**: Information Integration and Informatics
*   **NeTS**: Networking Technology and Systems
*   **RI**: Robust Intelligence
*   **SHF**: Software and Hardware Foundations

**Examples:**

*   Single program: **CSR**: Title
*   Collaborative Research: **Collaborative Research: RI**: Title (Research.gov will automatically prepend "Collaborative Research")
*   RUI eligible institution: **SHF: RUI**: Title
*   Interdisciplinary (most relevant first): **NeTS: AF**: Title

**Additional Supplementary Documents (Required):**

In the "Other Supplementary Documents" section, upload a document titled "Project Personnel and Partner Organizations" (required for all proposals; lead organization provides for all participants in collaborative proposals). This list must be numbered and include (in this order) Full name, Organization(s), and Role in the project, with each item separated by a semi-colon. Each person listed should start a new numbered line.

**Example:**

1.  Mei Lin; XYZ University; PI
2.  Jak Jabes; University of PQR; Senior/Key Personnel
3.  Jane Brown; XYZ University; Postdoctoral Researcher
4.  Rakel Ademas; ABC Inc.; Funded Consultant
5.  Maria Wan; Welldone Institution; Unfunded Collaborator
6.  Rimon Greene; ZZZ University; Subawardee

## 8. Tips for Successful Proposals

*   **Adhere to PAPPG**: Ensure your proposal meets the requirements specified in this solicitation and the applicable version of the NSF Proposal & Award Policies & Procedures Guide (PAPPG) for the relevant due date.
*   **Submit by Target Dates**: While proposals are accepted anytime, submitting by the target dates (September 11, 2025, and February 05, 2026, and annually thereafter) is highly encouraged to ensure consideration during the corresponding panel review cycle.
*   **Verify PI Limits**: Proposers are strongly encouraged to verify the dates of prior submissions to Future CoRe Programs for all personnel on their teams to avoid their proposals being returned without review due to exceeding the two-proposal limit within a 12-month period.
*   **Address Both Merit Review Criteria**: Fully address both the Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts criteria in your proposal, as both are given full consideration during the review process.
*   **Clear Goals and Assessment**: Include clearly stated goals, specific descriptions of activities, and a plan to document the outputs of those activities, especially for Broader Impacts.
*   **Interdisciplinary Research**: Proposals that extend beyond the scope of one Future CoRe program are welcome. Identify the most relevant program acronym first, followed by any other relevant program acronym(s) separated by colons in your proposal title.
*   **Broadening Participation**: NSF encourages proposals from EPSCoR-eligible institutions and collaborative proposals among EPSCoR and Non-EPSCoR-eligible jurisdictions led by EPSCoR institutions, to enhance engagement and promote sustainable growth in research enterprise.
*   **Timely Submission via Grants.gov**: If submitting via Grants.gov, NSF strongly recommends initiating proposal submission at least five business days in advance of a deadline to allow adequate time to address compliance errors and resubmissions.
